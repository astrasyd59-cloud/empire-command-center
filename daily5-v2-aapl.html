📚 Terminology Box

P/E Compression Originated: Graham & Dodd, 1934

Definition: When a stock's price-to-earnings multiple contracts despite earnings growth, indicating reduced market optimism.

Historical Context: Benjamin Graham and David Dodd formalized P/E analysis in Security Analysis (1934) after the 1929 crash exposed the dangers of paying excessive multiples. The term gained prominence in the 1970s Nifty Fifty era when growth stocks saw brutal multiple compression.

Why it matters: Even with perfect execution, investors lose money if they buy at peak multiples. AAPL's compression from 38x to 32x reflects shifting growth expectations.

Support & Resistance Originated: Charles Dow, 1900

Definition: Price levels where buying (support) or selling (resistance) pressure historically emerges, creating barriers to price movement.

Historical Context: Charles Dow, founder of The Wall Street Journal, observed that prices tended to reverse at certain psychological levels. His Dow Theory (1900) laid groundwork for technical analysis. Richard Wyckoff expanded this into the "price/volume" relationship in the 1930s.

Why it matters: AAPL's $245 support represents institutional accumulation. Breaking it signals a potential trend change.

Free Cash Flow (FCF) Popularized: 1980s LBO Era

Definition: Cash generated by operations minus capital expenditures—money available to return to shareholders.

Historical Context: FCF gained prominence during the 1980s leveraged buyout boom. KKR and other LBO firms used FCF to service acquisition debt. McKinsey's Valuation (1990) made it central to DCF analysis. Warren Buffett calls FCF "owner earnings."

Why it matters: AAPL's $107B FCF funds the largest capital return program in history. FCF yield (2.81%) vs. Treasury yields drives allocation decisions.

Beta (β) Developed: CAPM, 1964

Definition: A measure of a stock's volatility relative to the overall market. β = 1.21 means AAPL moves 1.21% for every 1% market move.

Historical Context: William Sharpe developed the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) in his 1964 PhD thesis at RAND Corporation. Beta became the cornerstone of Modern Portfolio Theory and the first "factor" in quantitative investing. The 2008 crisis exposed its limitations (correlations spike to 1.0).

Why it matters: AAPL's 1.21 beta makes it unsuitable for low-risk portfolios. It's a volatility amplifier in both directions.

Cash-Secured Put Options Exchange: 1973

Definition: Selling a put option while holding cash to purchase the stock if assigned—generating income or buying at a discount.

Historical Context: The Chicago Board Options Exchange (CBOE) opened in 1973 with 16 stocks. The Black-Scholes pricing model debuted the same year, legitimizing options as risk management tools. Cash-secured puts became popular in the 1990s as retail options trading expanded through online brokers.

Why it matters: Selling $230 AAPL puts generates 1.4% monthly income or acquires shares at a 9.1% discount to current price. The "wheel strategy" uses this for systematic income generation.

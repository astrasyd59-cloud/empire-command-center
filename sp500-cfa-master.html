📚 CFA Concept: Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

Level I · Chapter 41: Equity Valuation · LOS 41.a
Formula
P/E = Market Price per Share / Earnings per Share (EPS)

Where:
• P = Current market price per share
• E = Earnings per share (typically trailing 12 months)
• Result = How many dollars you pay for each dollar of earnings
Worked Example: Texas Pacific Land (TPL)
Given Data:
• Current Stock Price: $2,100 per share
• Trailing 12-Month EPS: $75.00
• Energy Sector Average P/E: 22x

Calculation:
P/E = $2,100 / $75.00 = 28.0x

Analysis:
TPL trades at 28x earnings vs sector average of 22x = 27% premium
Justified by: Unique Permian Basin land position (irreplaceable asset)
Concern: If oil prices fall, earnings decline magnifies P/E expansion
CFA Insight: High P/E can indicate growth expectations OR overvaluation. Always compare to sector peers and historical averages. TPL's premium is justified by asset scarcity, not growth — a distinction exam questions love to test.
Worked Example: Sandisk (SNDK) — High Growth
Given Data:
• Current Stock Price: $85.50
• Trailing EPS: $2.15
• Forward EPS (next 12 months): $4.50 (memory cycle recovery)
• Semiconductor Sector P/E: 18x

Trailing P/E Calculation:

📚 CFA Concept: Sharpe Ratio — Risk-Adjusted Returns

Level I · Chapter 57: Portfolio Risk and Return · LOS 57.c
Formula
Sharpe Ratio = (Rₚ − Rբ) / σₚ

Where:
• Rₚ = Portfolio (or stock) return
• Rբ = Risk-free rate (T-bill yield, typically 3-month)
• σₚ = Standard deviation of portfolio returns (volatility)
• Result = Excess return per unit of risk taken
Worked Example: Sandisk (SNDK) — YTD 2026
Given Data:
• YTD Return (Rₚ): 173.8%
• Risk-Free Rate (Rբ): 4.5% (3-month T-bill)
• Standard Deviation (σₚ): 45% (measured from daily returns)

Calculation:
Step 1: Calculate Excess Return
Rₚ − Rբ = 173.8% − 4.5% = 169.3%

Step 2: Divide by Volatility
Sharpe Ratio = 169.3 / 45 = 3.76

Benchmark Comparison:
• S&P 500 Sharpe (typical): ~0.8–1.2
• SNDK Sharpe: 3.76 ✓✓✓
• Interpretation: Exceptional risk-adjusted returns
CFA Grading Scale:
• < 0.5 = Poor (not compensating for risk)
• 0.5 – 1.0 = Acceptable
• 1.0 – 2.0 = Good
• > 2.0 = Excellent
• > 3.0 = Exceptional (rare, often unsustainable)
Worked Example: Intuit (INTU) — Negative Returns
Given Data:
• YTD Return: −42.55%
• Risk-Free Rate: 4.5%
• Standard Deviation: 28%

Calculation:
Excess Return = −42.55% − 4.5% = −47.05%
Sharpe Ratio = −47.05 / 28 = −1.68

Interpretation:
Negative Sharpe = Underperforming risk-free asset while taking risk.
You would have been better off in T-bills with zero volatility.

📚 CFA Concept: Expected Value — Probability-Weighted Outcomes

Level I · Chapter 2: Probability Concepts · LOS 2.d
Formula
E(X) = Σ [P(Xᵢ) × Xᵢ]

Where:
• E(X) = Expected value of random variable X
• P(Xᵢ) = Probability of outcome i occurring
• Xᵢ = Value of outcome i
• Σ = Sum across all possible outcomes
Worked Example: Moderna (MRNA) — Biotech Pipeline
Scenario Analysis:

Bull Case (30% probability):
• Cancer vaccine blockbuster approval
• Stock price target: $500
• Weighted value: 0.30 × $500 = $150

Base Case (50% probability):
• Steady COVID + flu vaccine sales
• Pipeline progress but no major catalyst
• Stock price: $200
• Weighted value: 0.50 × $200 = $100

Bear Case (20% probability):
• Clinical trial failures, revenue decline
• Stock price: $50
• Weighted value: 0.20 × $50 = $10

Expected Value Calculation:
E(X) = $150 + $100 + $10 = $260

Current Price: $180
Expected Return: ($260 − $180) / $180 = +44.4%
Decision Rule: If Expected Value > Current Price → Positive expected return.
MRNA shows 44% upside to probability-weighted fair value.
Key Risk: Biotech binary events — actual outcome is either $500 OR $50, not $260.

📚 CFA Concept: Position Sizing — Risk Management

Level I · Chapter 52: Portfolio Planning · LOS 52.b
Formula — Fixed Fractional Method
Position Size = (Portfolio Value × Risk %) / (Entry Price − Stop Loss)

Where:
• Portfolio Value = Total investable capital
• Risk % = Maximum loss willing to take (typically 1-2%)
• Entry Price = Purchase price per share
• Stop Loss = Price at which you exit for loss
• (Entry − Stop) = Risk per share
Worked Example: SNDK Position Sizing
Given:
• Portfolio Value: $100,000
• Risk Tolerance: 2% of portfolio = $2,000 max loss
• SNDK Entry Price: $85.50
• Technical Stop Loss: $77.00 (support level)

Calculation:
Step 1: Risk per share
$85.50 − $77.00 = $8.50 risk per share

Step 2: Number of shares
$2,000 / $8.50 = 235 shares (rounded down)

Step 3: Position Value
235 × $85.50 = $20,092

Result: 20% of portfolio in SNDK, with 2% max loss protected
Why This Matters: Without position sizing, a 20% position that drops 50% = 10% portfolio loss.
With proper sizing: Same 20% position with stop at −10% = 2% portfolio loss (your max).
CFA Rule: No single position should risk >2% of portfolio on any trade.

📚 CFA Concept: Beta — Systematic Risk Measurement

Level I · Chapter 57: Portfolio Risk and Return · LOS 57.a
Formula
βᵢ = Cov(Rᵢ, Rₘ) / σ²ₘ

Simplified Understanding:
• β = 1.0: Moves exactly with market
• β > 1.0: More volatile than market (magnifies moves)
• β < 1.0: Less volatile than market (dampens moves)
• β < 0: Inverse correlation (rare)
Worked Example: Portfolio Beta Calculation
Portfolio Construction:

TPL15%0.850.13
MRNA10%1.650.17
SPY (S&P 500)55%1.000.55
Portfolio Beta:
βₚ = 0.29 + 0.13 + 0.17 + 0.55 = 1.14

Interpretation:
If S&P 500 rises 10%, this portfolio expected to rise 11.4%.
If S&P 500 falls 10%, this portfolio expected to fall 11.4%.
14% more volatile than the market.
CAPM Connection (Chapter 57):
Expected Return = Rբ + β(Rₘ − Rբ)
With β = 1.14: You require 14% higher return than market to compensate for extra risk.

🎯 Key Takeaways for CFA Exam

  1. P/E Ratio: Compare to sector and historical average. Forward P/E matters more than trailing for growth stocks.
  2. Sharpe Ratio: >1.0 is good, >2.0 is excellent. Negative Sharpe = you'd prefer risk-free T-bills.
  3. Expected Value: Probability-weighted outcomes. Biotech = extreme example of binary outcomes.
  4. Position Sizing: Never risk >2% of portfolio on any single trade. Formula: (Portfolio × Risk%) / (Entry − Stop).
  5. Beta: Portfolio beta = weighted average. β > 1 = more volatile, requires higher expected return.
  6. Multiple Compression: Stock can fall even if earnings grow. P/E contraction hurts returns.

📚 CFA Lens: P/E Compression & Capital Allocation

Why These Metrics Matter for AAPL: Apple sits on $162B in cash but trades at a premium multiple. Understanding how they allocate that capital (buybacks vs R&D) tells you if the multiple is justified.

1. P/E Ratio (Chapter 41: Equity Valuation)

P/E = Market Price / Earnings Per Share
AAPL P/E = $247.10 / $7.62 = 32.4x
Context: Apple's P/E has compressed from 38x in January (when AI optimism peaked) to 32x now.

Why This Matters: At 32x, you're paying $32 for every $1 of earnings. Compare this to: • Microsoft: 29x (more consistent growth) • S&P 500 average: 21x
Apple trades at a 54% premium to the market. For that to work, Apple needs to grow earnings 15%+ annually—doable, but not guaranteed.

2. Free Cash Flow Yield (Chapter 28: Financial Analysis)

FCF Yield = Free Cash Flow Per Share / Stock Price
AAPL FCF Yield = $8.15 / $247.10 = 3.3%
Historical Context: FCF yield measures cash generation relative to price. Since 2015, Apple has averaged 4.5%—meaning you're getting less cash bang for your buck today than historically.

What They Do With It: Apple returns 90%+ of FCF to shareholders via buybacks ($95B annually) and dividends. This is why the stock has risen 400% since 2015 despite earnings only doubling—share count dropped by 35%.

3. Return on Equity (Chapter 28: Financial Analysis)

ROE = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity
AAPL ROE = $101B / $62B = 162%
Wait, What? ROE over 100% seems impossible. Here's the trick: Apple has negative equity because they've bought back so much stock (treasury stock reduces equity).

The Real Story: Apple's ROIC (Return on Invested Capital) is ~25%—still exceptional. They generate 25 cents for every dollar invested in the business. This is why Buffett loves it.

📖 Terminology Used in This Section
P/E Multiple Compression
Used when discussing AAPL's drop from 38x to 32x P/E

When a stock's price falls even if earnings stay flat because investors are willing to pay less for each dollar of earnings. Think of it as the market's "enthusiasm discount." First tracked systematically by Benjamin Graham in the 1930s. For growth stocks like Apple, a 6-point P/E compression = ~18% stock price drop without any fundamental deterioration.

Free Cash Flow (FCF)
Used when analyzing Apple's $95B annual buybacks

Cash generated by the business minus capital expenditures. Unlike earnings, FCF can't be fudged with accounting tricks. Apple generates $100B+ annually—real money they can return to you. Concept popularized by Warren Buffett in his 1986 letter to shareholders as the true measure of business value.

Return on Equity (ROE)
Used when showing Apple's 162% ROE (due to negative equity)

Net income divided by shareholders' equity. Measures how efficiently management uses invested capital. Developed by DuPont in the 1920s (yes, the chemical company). A 20%+ ROE is considered excellent; Apple's is distorted because buybacks have reduced equity below zero.

50-Day / 200-Day Moving Average
Used in trading levels section

The average stock price over the last 50 or 200 trading days. When price is above = uptrend; below = downtrend. The "Death Cross" (50 below 200) predicted the 1929, 2000, and 2008 crashes. First calculated by hand on Wall Street in the 1900s; now instant via Bloomberg.

Put Selling (Income Strategy)
Used in Trading Idea #2

Selling someone else the right to sell you stock at a specific price. You collect premium upfront. If stock stays above strike, you keep the premium. If it drops below, you buy the stock at the strike price (minus premium received). Buffett has used this to acquire Coca-Cola and American Express at discounts. Requires options approval from your broker.

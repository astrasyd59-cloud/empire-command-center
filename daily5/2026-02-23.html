CFA Lens: Valuation Methodologies

1. Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
P/E = Market Price per Share / Earnings per Share
Worked Example (JPM):
P/E = $310.79 / $20.01 = 15.53x

Interpretation: JPM trades at 15.53 times trailing earnings, below the S&P 500 Financials sector average of ~18x. Per CFA Institute guidelines, P/E is most meaningful when comparing firms within the same sector with similar growth profiles and capital structures.
2. Return on Equity (ROE) — DuPont Analysis
ROE = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity
ROE = (Net Income/Sales) × (Sales/Assets) × (Assets/Equity)
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Financial Leverage
Worked Example (AAPL):
Net Income (TTM): $123.4B | Equity: $77.1B
ROE = $123.4B / $77.1B = 160.1%

DuPont Decomposition:
• Net Margin: 24.2% (high-margin services mix)
• Asset Turnover: 0.87x (capital-light model)
• Leverage: 7.62x (aggressive share buybacks reduce equity base)

CFA Insight: Apple's astronomical ROE is artificially inflated by its negative equity position from treasury stock. CFA candidates should adjust for buyback impact when comparing ROE across time periods.
3. Dividend Discount Model (DDM) — Gordon Growth
V₀ = D₁ / (r - g)
Where: D₁ = D₀ × (1 + g), r = required return, g = perpetual growth rate
Worked Example (XOM Valuation):
• Current Dividend (D₀): $4.12
• Estimated Growth (g): 3.5% (inflation + productivity)
• Required Return (r): 8.0% (CAPM derived)

D₁ = $4.12 × 1.035 = $4.26
V₀ = $4.26 / (0.08 - 0.035) = $4.26 / 0.045 = $94.67

Observation: The DDM implies XOM is overvalued at $147.28 vs. model value of $94.67. However, DDM assumes perpetual dividend growth—problematic for cyclical energy firms. CFA curriculum emphasizes using multi-stage models for such companies.

Terminology: Historical Context

Beta (β)
A measure of a stock's volatility in relation to the overall market. A beta of 1.0 indicates the stock moves with the market; less than 1.0 means lower volatility; greater than 1.0 indicates higher volatility.
Origin: Introduced by William Sharpe in his 1964 Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) paper, building on Harry Markowitz's Modern Portfolio Theory. The term derives from the second letter of the Greek alphabet, signifying its role as the slope coefficient in regression analysis.
P/E Ratio
Price-to-Earnings ratio compares a company's share price to its earnings per share. It indicates how much investors are willing to pay per dollar of earnings, serving as a primary valuation benchmark.
Origin: Popularized by Benjamin Graham in "The Intelligent Investor" (1949). Graham considered stocks with P/E below 15 as potentially undervalued. The Shiller P/E (CAPE), developed by Robert Shiller in 1988, uses 10-year average earnings to smooth cyclical variations.
Return on Equity (ROE)
Net income divided by shareholders' equity, expressed as a percentage. ROE measures how effectively management uses invested capital to generate profits. The DuPont decomposition breaks ROE into profit margin, asset turnover, and financial leverage.
Origin: The DuPont Corporation pioneered ROE decomposition in the 1910s. Donaldson Brown, a DuPont explosives salesman turned financial executive, developed the formula in 1914 to evaluate acquisition targets. It became the cornerstone of DuPont's financial control system.
Risk/Reward Ratio
A metric comparing the potential profit of a trade to its potential loss. Calculated by dividing the distance from entry to target by the distance from entry to stop-loss. Professional traders typically seek ratios of 1:2 or higher.
Origin: Formalized in trading literature by Jesse Livermore in "Reminiscences of a Stock Operator" (1923). Livermore emphasized cutting losses quickly and letting winners run. The mathematical formulation gained prominence with the rise of algorithmic trading in the 1980s.
Market Capitalization
The total dollar value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated by multiplying share price by shares outstanding. Categories include: Large-cap ($10B+), Mid-cap ($2B-$10B), Small-cap ($300M-$2B), and Micro-cap (below $300M).
Origin: The concept emerged in the 1960s with the rise of institutional investing. Standard & Poor's formally introduced market-cap-based indices in 1957 with the S&P 500. The term gained widespread use in the 1980s as index investing became mainstream.

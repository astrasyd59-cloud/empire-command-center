📊 Options Strategy: Cash-Secured Puts
Income Generation · Risk-Defined · CFA Level II Prep

Strategy Mechanics

Sell out-of-the-money puts on stocks you want to own. Collect premium immediately. If assigned, own stock at effective price (strike - premium). If not assigned, keep premium as income.

Risk Profile

Maximum loss occurs if stock goes to zero: (Strike × 100) - Premium. Breakeven at strike - premium. Requires cash collateral equal to strike × 100 per contract.

Payoff Diagram: Short $95 WMT Put @ $2.50 Max Profit: $250 | Breakeven: $92.50
CFA Lens: Option Greeks
Delta (Δ): Price sensitivity to underlying ($ change per $1 move) Theta (Θ): Time decay (premium lost per day) Implied Volatility: Market's expected future volatility

Short puts have positive Delta (bullish) and positive Theta (benefit from time decay). Select strikes with 30-45 DTE (days to expiration) and 0.30 Delta for optimal risk/reward.

CFA Spotlight — DuPont Analysis
🎯 CFA Spotlight: DuPont Analysis
Financial Analysis Techniques
Exam Weight: 10-12%
Why It Matters
DuPont analysis decomposes ROE into three drivers: profitability (margin), efficiency (turnover), and leverage. This reveals whether high ROE comes from operational excellence or financial engineering—critical for credit analysis and valuation.

Three-Step DuPont

ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier

Shows operational efficiency, asset use efficiency, and financial leverage contribution.

Five-Step DuPont

ROE = (EBIT/Revenue) × (EBT/EBIT) × (Revenue/Assets) × (Assets/Equity) × (Net Income/EBT)

Adds tax burden and interest burden analysis for deeper insight.

Exam Application

When comparing two companies with similar ROE, DuPont reveals quality differences. A company with 20% ROE from 15% margins and 1.3x turnover is superior to one with 20% ROE from 5% margins, 1.0x turnover, and 4.0x leverage. The leveraged firm faces higher financial risk.

Historical Context
The DuPont analysis was developed by Donaldson Brown at DuPont Corporation in 1914 to evaluate acquisition targets. It became the standard for financial analysis and remains a CFA curriculum cornerstone.

Question 1 of 5
A stock has a P/E of 20x and EPS of $5. If earnings grow 10% but the P/E compresses to 18x, what is the new stock price?
New EPS = $5 × 1.10 = $5.50
New Price = New EPS × New P/E = $5.50 × 18 = $99.00. This demonstrates P/E compression—earnings grew but the multiple contracted, limiting price appreciation.
Question 2 of 5
According to the CAPM, what is the expected return for a stock with β = 1.2, if Rf = 3% and Rm = 9%?
E(R) = Rf + β(Rm - Rf)
E(R) = 3% + 1.2(9% - 3%) = 3% + 7.2% = 10.2%. The stock's expected return exceeds the market due to its higher systematic risk (β > 1).
Question 3 of 5
In a covered interest rate parity scenario, if the domestic rate is 5%, foreign rate is 3%, and spot is 1.00, what is the approximate 1-year forward rate?
F = S × (1 + r domestic) / (1 + r foreign)
F = 1.00 × (1.05/1.03) = 1.0194. The high-interest currency (domestic) trades at a forward discount—agreement to buy domestic currency in the future is cheaper due to interest rate differential.
Question 4 of 5
Using the three-step DuPont analysis, a company has: Net Margin = 8%, Asset Turnover = 1.5x, Equity Multiplier = 2.0x. What is ROE?
ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier
ROE = 8% × 1.5 × 2.0 = 24%. The company achieves strong returns through a balance of profitability (8%), efficiency (1.5x turnover), and moderate leverage (2.0x).
Question 5 of 5
A trader risks $500 to make $1,500. How many trades can they lose in a row before breaking even if they win just 30% of the time?
Expected Value = (Win% × Avg Win) - (Loss% × Avg Loss)
With 3:1 reward-to-risk, expected value is (0.3 × $1,500) - (0.7 × $500) = $450 - $350 = +$100 per trade. Positive expectancy means profits over time regardless of individual streaks. Position sizing (2% rule) ensures survival through drawdowns.
Today's Glossary — 4 Terms · Financial Analysis
📖 Terms to Know
Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
Market price per share divided by earnings per share. Indicates how much investors pay per dollar of earnings. Trailing P/E uses historical earnings; Forward P/E uses analyst estimates.
Origin: Graham & Dodd, "Security Analysis" (1934)
Return on Equity (ROE)
Net income divided by average shareholders' equity. Measures profitability relative to book value. DuPont analysis decomposes ROE into margin, turnover, and leverage components.
Origin: DuPont Corporation, 1914 (Donaldson Brown)
Beta (β)
Measures systematic risk relative to the market. β = 1.0 means matching market volatility. β > 1.0 indicates higher volatility; β < 1.0 indicates lower volatility.
Origin: William Sharpe, CAPM (1964)
Interest Rate Parity
The forward exchange rate premium/discount equals the interest rate differential between two currencies. Prevents risk-free arbitrage in forex markets.
Origin: Keynes (1923), formalized by Einzig (1937)

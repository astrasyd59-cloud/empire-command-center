Long Call Strategy
Profit/Loss
    │
  ▲ │              ╱
    │            ╱
  0 ├──────────●─────────→ Stock Price
    │         /│
 -$ │        / │
    │       /  │ Premium Paid
    │      /   │
    │     /    │
        Strike  Breakeven = Strike + Premium
Worked Example:
AAPL Feb 28 $270 Call @ $4.50 premium Max Loss = Premium Paid = $4.50 per share ($450 per contract) Breakeven = Strike + Premium = $270 + $4.50 = $274.50 Max Gain = Unlimited (theoretically) At expiration: - If AAPL = $280 → Profit = $280 - $274.50 = $5.50 ($550/contract) - If AAPL = $270 → Loss = -$4.50 ($450/contract) - If AAPL = $274.50 → Breakeven = $0
The Greeks (First-Order Sensitivities):
Delta (Δ): ∂OptionPrice/∂Underlying → Price sensitivity to $1 move in stock Gamma (Γ): ∂Delta/∂Underlying → Rate of change of Delta (convexity) Theta (Θ): ∂OptionPrice/∂Time → Daily time decay (always negative for long options) Vega (V): ∂OptionPrice/∂Volatility → Sensitivity to 1% change in implied vol
"Options are a zero-sum game with a negative expected return due to transaction costs and bid-ask spreads. The edge comes from better forecasting of realized volatility versus implied." — CFA Curriculum, Derivatives
CFA Level I Quiz 5 Questions
Q1. A portfolio has an expected return of 12% and standard deviation of 18%. The risk-free rate is 4%. What is the Sharpe Ratio? Portfolio Management
Answer: 0.44
Sharpe Ratio = (Portfolio Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation
= (12% - 4%) / 18% = 8% / 18% = 0.444... ≈ 0.44
Q2. A bond with a 5% coupon trades at a yield of 6%. What can you conclude about its price? Fixed Income
Answer: The bond trades at a discount (below par)
When Coupon Rate < Yield → Price < Par (Discount Bond)
When Coupon Rate > Yield → Price > Par (Premium Bond)
When Coupon Rate = Yield → Price = Par
Q3. If the USD/EUR rate is 1.10, what is the EUR/USD rate? Economics
Answer: 0.9091
EUR/USD = 1 / (USD/EUR) = 1 / 1.10 = 0.9091
This is the reciprocal rate conversion essential for FX calculations.
Q4. A stock's beta is 1.5. The market returns 10% and the risk-free rate is 3%. What is the expected return per CAPM? Equity Investments
Answer: 13.5%
CAPM: E(R) = Rf + β × (Rm - Rf)
E(R) = 3% + 1.5 × (10% - 3%)
E(R) = 3% + 1.5 × 7% = 3% + 10.5% = 13.5%
Q5. Current ratio is 2.0 and quick ratio is 1.2. What does this imply about inventory? Financial Reporting
Answer: Inventory represents 40% of current assets
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities = 2.0
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities = 1.2
Difference: 2.0 - 1.2 = 0.8 → Inventory/CL = 0.8
Since CA = 2.0 × CL, then Inventory = 0.8 × CL = 0.4 × CA → 40%
Glossary 4 Terms
Duration
Measures bond price sensitivity to yield changes. Modified Duration = Macaulay Duration / (1 + y). A duration of 5 means ~5% price change for 1% yield move.
Convexity
Second-order measure of price-yield relationship. Positive convexity benefits investors—bonds gain more when yields fall than they lose when yields rise.
Carry Trade
Borrowing in low-yield currency to invest in high-yield currency. Profitable when interest rate differential exceeds FX movement. Risk: Sudden unwinds (e.g., JPY in 2007).
Quantitative Easing
Central bank purchases of securities to increase money supply and lower long-term rates. First implemented by BOJ (2001), later Fed (2008), ECB (2015).

Options Theory & Mechanics

🔧 Put Selling Strategy Mechanics

A short put position involves selling the right (but not the obligation) for another party to sell the underlying asset to you at a predetermined strike price. The seller receives a premium upfront and assumes the obligation to purchase the underlying if assigned.

Short Put Payoff at Expiry = min(0, ST - K) + Premium Received

Where: ST = Spot price at expiry, K = Strike price
💡 Learning Point

Why sell puts? Put selling is a bullish to neutral strategy that capitalizes on time decay (theta) and potentially elevated implied volatility. The seller profits if: (1) the underlying stays above the strike, or (2) implied volatility decreases. It's commonly used to generate income or to acquire stocks at a discount to current prices.

🛡️ Risk Mitigation Strategies

✅ Stop-Loss (Buy-to-Close)

Close position if premium doubles (A$1,386) or loss reaches 200% of max profit. This limits loss while preserving most of the profit potential.

⚡ Roll Management

If tested, roll down and out: move to lower strike and/or further expiry to collect additional premium and improve breakeven.

🔄 Hedge with Put Spread

Buy deeper OTM put to create a put credit spread. Reduces max loss but also reduces premium collected by hedge cost.

Thought Nuggets & CFA Curriculum Links

1
Derivatives

The Asymmetric Nature of Option Selling

Short put strategies exhibit negative skew—frequent small gains punctuated by occasional large losses. This is the opposite of lottery-ticket buying (long options). CFA candidates must recognize that Sharpe ratios can be misleading for negatively skewed strategies; Sortino ratios or Omega ratios better capture tail risk. The insurance analogy is apt: you collect premiums until the "disaster" strikes.

2
Portfolio Management

Position Sizing and the Kelly Criterion

The Kelly Criterion suggests optimal bet sizing based on win probability and payoff ratios. For this trade with ~80% POP and 1:2.25 risk-reward, the Kelly fraction suggests 2.5% allocation. However, half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly is prudent due to estimation errors in probability and the non-ergodic nature of option outcomes. CFA Level III emphasizes that position sizing often matters more than entry timing.

3
Fixed Income

Volatility as an Asset Class

Selling volatility is economically similar to selling insurance or writing CDS protection—you collect a premium for assuming tail risk. The volatility risk premium (VRP) exists because investors systematically overpay for downside protection (put skew). VRP averages 2-4% annually in equity indices. This trade monetizes that premium, analogous to collecting carry in fixed income.

4
Behavioral Finance

The Disposition Effect and Early Closure

Research shows traders tend to close winners too early (realizing gains) and hold losers too long (disposition effect). For short options, the optimal management is often counter-intuitive: close at 25-50% of max profit rather than holding to expiry. The "rule of 50%" captures most theta decay while eliminating tail risk in the final weeks.

Quantitative Methods

Monte Carlo Simulation for Scenario Analysis

A proper risk assessment requires Monte Carlo simulation—generating thousands of price paths using Geometric Brownian Motion. For DAX puts, incorporating realistic parameters (μ ≈ 6%, σ ≈ 18%) reveals that the "80% POP" headline masks significant tail risk. In 10,000 simulations, expect ~5-7 breakeven breaches per 100 trades, with 1-2 producing 2x+ losses.

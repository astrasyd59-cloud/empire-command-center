1. Understanding the GENIUS Act

The Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S. Stablecoins Act (GENIUS Act) became law in 2025, establishing the first comprehensive federal regulatory framework for payment stablecoins in the United States.

Key Provisions

  • Federal Authority: Grants the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) primary regulatory authority over domestic stablecoin issuers
  • Reserve Requirements: Mandates 1:1 backing with high-quality liquid assets (Treasuries, cash equivalents)
  • Capital Requirements: Establishes minimum capital standards for issuers based on issuance volume
  • Custody Controls: Requires robust segregation and protection of customer assets
  • Redemption Rights: Guarantees 1:1 redemption at par value
  • Yield Prohibition: Explicitly bans stablecoin issuers from paying yield or interest

The February 2026 OCC Proposed Rulemaking

In February 2026, the OCC published a 376-page proposed rulemaking to implement the GENIUS Act. While the Act itself was relatively straightforward, the OCC's interpretation has introduced significant uncertainty—particularly regarding how the yield prohibition applies to arrangements between issuers and third parties.

"The proposed rules go beyond what many in the industry expected, creating a rebuttable presumption that yield payments through intermediaries may constitute attempts to evade the statutory prohibition." — CoinDesk Regulatory Analysis, February 2026

2. The Yield Controversy

The most contentious aspect of the OCC's proposed rules involves the interpretation of the GENIUS Act's yield prohibition. What seemed like a straightforward ban on issuer-paid yield has evolved into a potential industry-wide restructuring requirement.

Industry Assumptions vs. OCC Interpretation

Industry Assumption (Pre-February 2026)

  • The GENIUS Act prohibits issuers from paying yield
  • Third parties (exchanges, lending platforms) could continue offering yield/rewards on stablecoins
  • Coinbase, BlockFi, and similar programs would remain viable
  • Clear separation between issuer and yield provider preserves legality

OCC's Proposed Interpretation

  • Creates a "rebuttable presumption" that yield payments through intermediaries are attempts to evade the ban
  • Requires companies to demonstrate true arm's length relationships
  • Suggests revenue-sharing or similar arrangements between issuers and yield providers may violate the spirit of the Act
  • Shifts burden of proof to companies to show arrangements are legitimate

What "Rebut the Presumption" Means

Companies can maintain third-party yield arrangements, but must provide evidence demonstrating:

  • The third party is independently funding yield payments from its own resources
  • No revenue-sharing, fee arrangements, or other compensation flows from issuer to yield provider
  • The arrangement represents genuine commercial activity, not a structuring attempt
  • Complete documentation of the economic substance of the relationship
"The presumption creates a challenging compliance environment. Firms must now maintain extensive documentation showing their yield programs are genuinely independent of issuer arrangements—a significant operational burden." — Legal Analysis, Crypto Trading Compliance

Coinbase

Exchange

Exposure: High

Circle (USDC)

Issuer

Exposure: Critical

Relationship with Coinbase and other exchanges under microscope. Revenue-sharing models may need fundamental restructuring. Second-largest stablecoin by market cap.

PayPal / Paxos (PYUSD)

Issuer + Platform

Exposure: Medium-High

White-label arrangement between PayPal and Paxos creates unique structural questions. Vertical integration model may face heightened scrutiny.

Tether (USDT)

Issuer

Exposure: Low-Medium

Limited yield arrangements to date. However, international issuer status may create separate regulatory questions under GENIUS Act foreign provisions.

BlockFi / Lending Platforms

Yield Providers

Exposure: High

Core business model dependent on stablecoin yield products. Must prove independent funding sources. Potential model transformation required.

Traditional Banks

Competitors

Exposure: Opportunity

Banking lobby supported yield restrictions to prevent stablecoin competition with deposits. May gain market share if crypto yield products are constrained.

"The banking industry's opposition to stablecoin yield has been a driving force behind restrictive interpretations. Banks view yield-bearing stablecoins as direct competitors to deposit products." — Policy Analysis, CoinDesk

4. Trading Operations Implications

For trading firms and market makers, the GENIUS Act OCC rules create several operational considerations that require immediate attention.

💼 Prime Brokerage

  • Yield-bearing stablecoin products in prime brokerage stacks require immediate compliance review
  • Documentation requirements for "arm's length" relationships will increase operational overhead
  • Counterparty due diligence must now include GENIUS Act compliance assessments
  • Potential margin compression if yield offerings are constrained

⚖️ Compliance Requirements

  • Enhanced Know-Your-Counterparty (KYC) for stablecoin yield arrangements
  • Documentation standards for proving arm's length relationships
  • Regular attestation requirements may become standard
  • Integration of GENIUS Act checks into existing compliance workflows

📊 Treasury Operations

  • Cash management strategies using stablecoin yield need reassessment
  • Alternative yield sources may command premium
  • Reserve requirement changes could affect stablecoin liquidity
  • Redemption mechanisms require updated operational procedures

🔄 Trading Strategies

  • Basis trades involving yield-bearing stablecoins face uncertainty
  • Funding rate arbitrage may shift to non-yield instruments
  • Stablecoin-collateralized positions need repricing
  • Alternative stablecoins may gain preference based on yield treatment

Immediate Action Items for Trading Firms

  1. Audit all current stablecoin yield arrangements and counterparty relationships
  2. Document the economic substance of any third-party yield programs
  3. Assess prime brokerage stablecoin products for compliance exposure
  4. Engage legal counsel on "rebuttable presumption" defense preparation
  5. Consider submitting public comments during OCC comment period
  6. Model scenarios for yield-constrained environment impact on P&L

6. Relationship to the Clarity Act

The GENIUS Act is not the only major crypto legislation in play. The Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century Act (FIT21, commonly called the "Clarity Act") represents broader market structure legislation still pending in Congress.

Key Differences

GENIUS Act Narrow focus on payment stablecoins Already law (2025) OCC rulemaking underway
Clarity Act Broader market structure + securities framework Pending in Congress Multiple agency jurisdictions

The Yield Negotiation Connection

Stablecoin yield has emerged as a major sticking point in Clarity Act negotiations:

  • Crypto industry advocates want explicit authorization for third-party yield arrangements
  • Banking lobby opposes any language that legitimizes stablecoin yield competing with deposits
  • OCC's aggressive interpretation may remove the need for Congressional action on yield specifically
  • However, Clarity Act still needed for broader securities classification and exchange registration issues

Trump Ethics and AML/KYC Issues

While the OCC action may resolve the yield question, other Clarity Act issues remain unresolved:

  • Trump Ethics Concerns: Potential conflicts of interest given former President Trump's crypto ventures
  • AML/KYC Standards: Anti-money laundering and know-your-customer requirements still being negotiated
  • Securities Classification: Which tokens constitute securities remains a key debate
"OCC's yield interpretation may effectively take the issue off the table for Congress. If the regulator has already imposed strict limits, legislators may see less urgency to address it in the Clarity Act." — Legislative Analysis, CoinDesk

7. Implementation Timeline

2025

GENIUS Act Signed Into Law

Congress passes and President signs the first federal stablecoin regulatory framework, granting OCC authority over domestic issuers.

February 2026

OCC Proposed Rulemaking Published

376-page proposed rules released, including controversial yield interpretation creating rebuttable presumption against third-party arrangements.

March – May 2026

Public Comment Period

Industry stakeholders can submit comments on proposed rules. Coinbase, Circle, and others expected to lobby for narrower interpretation.

Q3 2026

Comment Review & Analysis

OCC reviews public comments. Significant industry pushback may result in modifications to yield provisions.

Q4 2026

Final Rulemaking Expected

Target date for finalized rules. Implementation timeline typically 6-12 months after publication.

Ongoing

Clarity Act Legislative Process

If Clarity Act passes before final OCC rules, the agency must revise to conform with Congressional intent.

⚠️ Key Uncertainty

The interaction between OCC rulemaking and potential Clarity Act passage creates significant timing uncertainty. Firms should prepare for multiple scenarios:

  • Scenario A: OCC finalizes strict rules before Clarity Act passes → Yield restrictions take effect 2027
  • Scenario B: Clarity Act passes with yield provisions → OCC must revise rules → Delays implementation
  • Scenario C: Compromise interpretation emerges → Moderate restrictions with clear compliance paths
